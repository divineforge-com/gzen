## Observation

In fast-paced modern life, we constantly seek "certainty." We draft ten-year roadmaps for our careers, set rigid growth targets for our investments, expect relationships to remain unchanged, and hope our physical and mental health stays forever at its peak.

Yet, change always arrives unannounced. Market shifts shatter business plans, unexpected events disrupt daily routines, and the passage of time brings a decline in physical energy. When external events do not align with our expectations, we react instinctively with resistance, anxiety, and suffering. We squeeze our hands tighter to hold on to slipping sand, only to grow increasingly exhausted.

This suffering does not arise from change itself, but from our attempt to grab a static illusion in a world that is inherently in constant flux.

---

## Principle

The I Ching says: \"Adapting to changes prevents exhaustion.\" It also states: \"When a path is exhausted, change; with change, path opens; through openness, it endures.\"

This directly aligns with the Buddha's teachings on impermanence (Anicca) and non-self (Anatta). The world is composed of causes and conditions that continuously aggregate and disperse. When the conditions you rely on change, but you cling tightly to old patterns (exhaustion), suffering arises. However, if you observe the shifts in conditions and adjust your actions accordingly (change), a new path opens up (openness).

The essence of adaptation is **releasing attachment to a fixed self and specific outcomes**. Just as water flows around a rock or fills a depression, it has no fixed shape, and therefore can never be broken. In the I Ching, this is called \"Sui Shi\" — acting in harmony with the timing.

When we understand adaptation, we do not rush to harvest in the spring when we should be sowing, nor do we expand blindly in the winter when we should be conserving. We learn to act with the strength of Qián (Heaven) and to rest in the receptivity of Kūn (Earth).

---

## Application

In daily life, you can practice adaptation and peace through these three steps:

1. **Observe Your Resistance**
   When plans are disrupted or things take an unexpected turn, notice your physical and emotional reactions. Is your chest tight? Are you feeling angry or frustrated? This is a sign of resistance against change. Take a deep breath and tell yourself: "Change has occurred. This is the reality of the present moment."

2. **Identify Your Time and Position (时位 - Shí Wèi)**
   Calm your mind and observe your current situation just as you would observe the seasons. Ask yourself: What phase am I in right now?
   - Is this a phase for quiet accumulation and waiting? (Like Gèn/Mountain, or the hidden dragon in Qián)
   - Is this a phase for taking decisive action and overcoming obstacles? (Like Qián/Heaven, or Zhèn/Thunder)
   - Is this a phase for listening, yielding, and supporting? (Like Kūn/Earth, or Xùn/Wind)
   Acting in harmony with your position saves immense energy.

3. **Rest in the Constant (不易 - Bù Yì) Awareness**
   No matter how turbulent the external changes (变易) are, return to the still point within. Your thoughts change, emotions change, age changes, but the "awareness" that observes these changes never changes. In your daily meditation or breath counting, practice resting in this unchanging awareness.
